# Unsupervised matrix factorization for biological program discovery: methods, plasma proteomics, and the road ahead

**The field of unsupervised latent program discovery sits at a striking inflection point.** Methods like consensus NMF, topic models, and MOFA have transformed single-cell transcriptomics by revealing gene expression programs, cell states, and biological activities without supervised labels. Yet these same methods remain almost entirely unapplied to one of the most data-rich frontiers in human biology: population-scale plasma proteomics. The supervised organ-clock paradigm introduced by Oh et al. (Nature 2023) has rapidly become dominant for interpreting plasma proteomic aging, spawning dozens of replication studies — but a genuinely unsupervised decomposition of the plasma proteome into latent biological programs has never been published. This review systematically compares factorization methods across data modalities, examines the organ-clock literature through early 2026, identifies emerging analytical frameworks, catalogs technical failure modes, and maps the critical gaps where unsupervised decomposition could reshape our understanding of circulating protein biology.

## How NMF, topic models, and factor analysis compare across biological data

The core methods differ fundamentally in their statistical assumptions, and these assumptions determine which data modalities they suit. **Non-negative matrix factorization (NMF)** decomposes a data matrix V ≈ WH under the constraint that all entries in W and H are non-negative, producing additive, parts-based representations naturally suited to count data like scRNA-seq. Consensus NMF (cNMF), developed by Kotliar, Veres, and colleagues (eLife 2019), stabilizes NMF by running it hundreds of times with different random initializations, clustering the resulting components, and selecting consensus medians. In head-to-head benchmarks on simulated scRNA-seq data, **cNMF outperformed ICA, LDA, and PCA** at recovering both cell-type identity programs and cell-activity programs such as cell cycle and hypoxia response. Its non-negativity constraint yields gene program spectra in TPM units — directly interpretable as expression levels — and usage matrices readable as fractional program activity per cell.

**Latent Dirichlet allocation (LDA)** and its modern implementations (CountClust, fastTopics) treat each cell as a "document" and each gene's counts as "words," discovering "topics" that represent gene expression programs. A landmark result by Carbonetto, Sarkar, and Stephens (arXiv 2021; updated through 2026) formally proved that **multinomial topic models and Poisson NMF yield identical maximum-likelihood estimates**, differing only in parameterization. This equivalence means fastTopics' coordinate-descent algorithms for Poisson NMF dramatically outperform traditional EM-based LDA fitting — producing better fits in less time. For raw count data, this makes the distinction between "NMF" and "topic modeling" largely algorithmic rather than statistical.

**Independent component analysis (ICA)** takes a different approach: it seeks statistically independent source signals rather than non-negative parts. The Cantini et al. benchmark (Bioinformatics 2019) across **26 cancer transcriptomic datasets** found that stabilized ICA (sICA) produced more reproducible components across independent cohorts than standard NMF or PCA for bulk transcriptomics. ICA's strength lies in separating independent biological processes, but its components can have negative weights, complicating biological interpretation.

**MOFA/MOFA+** (Argelaguet et al., Molecular Systems Biology 2018; Genome Biology 2020) occupies a unique niche as a Bayesian multi-omics factor analysis framework. It decomposes multiple data modalities simultaneously, using automatic relevance determination (ARD) priors to automatically prune irrelevant factors and spike-and-slab priors for feature-level sparsity. MOFA handles missing data natively, supports Gaussian, Poisson, and Bernoulli observation models, and — crucially — imposes **no non-negativity constraint**, making it applicable to continuous, potentially negative data like Olink NPX values. Its multi-group extension explicitly models batch structure by allowing factors to have different activity across sample groups.

**Archetypal analysis** finds extreme points on the data's convex hull, representing specialized cell states as Pareto-optimal trade-offs between biological tasks. The ACTION/ACTIONet framework (Mohammadi et al., Nature Communications 2018, 2020) combines separable NMF with archetypal analysis for multiresolution cell-state decomposition. The deep archetypal analysis method MIDAA (Genome Biology 2025) extends this to multi-omics with GPU acceleration, handling ~100K cells in ~5 minutes.

**Deep generative models** — scVI, totalVI, MultiVI — achieve superior scalability (>1M cells) and built-in batch correction via variational autoencoders with negative binomial likelihoods. However, their nonlinear decoder creates a **black-box latent space** that sacrifices the direct gene-program interpretability of linear methods. The linearly-decoded VAE (LDVAE, Svensson et al. 2020) and newer FactVAE (Wang et al., Briefings in Bioinformatics 2025) bridge this gap by using nonlinear encoders with linear or factorized decoders, preserving interpretability while gaining representational power.

**Spectra** (Kunes et al., Nature Biotechnology 2024) represents a semi-supervised evolution: it incorporates prior gene-set knowledge as a gene-gene graph while simultaneously discovering novel programs from residual variation. In breast cancer immunotherapy data, Spectra disentangled CD8+ T cell exhaustion from tumor reactivity — a discrimination that unsupervised NMF alone struggled to achieve.

## The supervised organ-clock paradigm and what it misses

Oh, Rutledge, and colleagues in the Wyss-Coray lab established the dominant framework for interpreting plasma proteomic aging in their Nature 2023 paper. Using SomaScan aptamer-based measurements of ~5,000 proteins across 5,676 adults, they assigned proteins to **11 organs** (brain, heart, vasculature, lung, kidney, liver, intestine, muscle, immune system, adipose, pancreas) using GTEx tissue expression data — specifically, proteins whose encoding genes showed ≥4-fold enrichment in one tissue. LASSO regression models trained on each organ-specific protein subset predicted chronological age, and the residual "age gap" became the organ-specific aging biomarker. Approximately **20% of the population** showed strongly accelerated aging in at least one organ, and organ age acceleration conferred 20–50% higher mortality risk.

This framework has been replicated and extended at remarkable speed. Oh et al. (Nature Medicine 2025) scaled the approach from SomaScan to **Olink Explore 3072 in 44,498 UK Biobank participants**, finding that brain age acceleration conferred a hazard ratio of **3.1 for Alzheimer's disease** — comparable to carrying one APOE ε4 allele. Goeminne et al. (Cell Metabolism 2025) from the Gladyshev lab independently replicated the organ-specific approach using elastic net regularization in >50,000 UK Biobank subjects, adding mortality-based organ models as a methodological advance. Wang et al. (Nature Aging 2026) developed 10 organ clocks validated across the UK Biobank, China Kadoorie Biobank (n=3,977), and US Nurses' Health Study (n=800), achieving cross-cohort correlations of r=0.93–0.98. Kivimäki et al. (Lancet Digital Health 2025) validated the framework in the Whitehall II and ARIC cohorts over 20-year follow-up.

The Wyss-Coray lab has continued producing high-impact work through early 2026: Farinas et al. (Nature Medicine 2025) analyzed **CSF–plasma protein balance** in 2,171 paired samples, finding that CSF-to-plasma ratios of 848 proteins increase with aging; Oh et al. (Nature Medicine 2025) developed a CSF synaptic biomarker (YWHAG:NPTX2 ratio) predicting cognitive decline; and the Global Neurodegeneration Proteomics Consortium paper (Nature Medicine 2025) reported ~250 million protein measurements from >35,000 biofluid samples.

Yet the supervised paradigm has fundamental limitations that unsupervised decomposition could address. **GTEx tissue labels impose anatomical boundaries that may not reflect circulating protein biology.** Many plasma proteins are secreted by multiple tissues, have unknown tissue origins, or participate in systemic processes (inflammation, ECM remodeling, complement activation, senescence-associated secretory phenotype) that cannot be cleanly assigned to a single organ. By pre-partitioning the proteome into organ-labeled subsets, the supervised approach fragments cross-organ programs — a factor capturing systemic inflammaging, for instance, would be split across the immune, liver, and vascular models rather than discovered as a coherent biological program.

The closest existing work to truly unsupervised plasma proteome decomposition is Coenen et al. (Frontiers in Aging 2023), who integrated four SomaScan datasets and used hierarchical clustering on LOESS-smoothed protein age trajectories to identify 15 temporal clusters. Lehallier et al. (Nature Medicine 2019) similarly clustered protein trajectories, discovering the famous "waves" of proteomic change at ages ~34, ~60, and ~78. But **neither study applied matrix factorization or topic models** — they clustered proteins by trajectory shape rather than decomposing the sample-by-protein matrix into latent programs with per-individual usage scores.

## One VAE study and one MOFA study begin to fill the gap

Two recent studies represent the first attempts at unsupervised latent factor discovery from population-scale plasma proteomics. Zhuang, Smith, and Diogo (medRxiv 2024, Takeda) applied a **variational autoencoder to Olink data from 51,180 UK Biobank participants**, identifying 36 protein modules via Leiden clustering in the VAE latent space. These modules corresponded to major cell types (hepatocytes, platelets, immune cells, adipocytes) and biological processes (complement, coagulation, inflammation), with module abundances associated with disease status and genetic variants. The authors explicitly chose a VAE over NMF, citing the need to capture non-linear relationships in the data.

A separate study (medRxiv 2025) applied **MOFA to integrate plasma proteins, metabolites, biochemistry, and haematology** from 46,081 UK Biobank individuals, identifying 20 latent factors that explained 81.8% of metabolite variance and 36.8% of proteomic variance, with **400 significant associations with 111 incident diseases**. Multi-omic MOFA models for type 2 diabetes achieved a C-index of 0.865, significantly outperforming single clinical biomarkers.

These studies are groundbreaking but leave critical ground uncovered. Neither applied NMF, cNMF, or topic models — the methods with the strongest track record for discovering interpretable biological programs in transcriptomics. Neither attempted cross-platform validation (e.g., discovering programs in Olink and validating in SomaScan or mass spectrometry). And neither directly compared their unsupervised programs with the supervised organ-clock factors on the same data.

## Emerging frameworks connect factorization to causal inference and transfer learning

The most exciting methodological frontier combines learned latent factors with causal reasoning. Zhang et al. (European Journal of Epidemiology 2026) introduced **MVMR-BFA**, integrating Bayesian factor analysis directly into multivariable Mendelian randomization to address multicollinearity when testing many correlated molecular exposures — precisely the scenario with NMF-derived protein programs. They applied it to DNA methylation modules and mitochondrial DNA copy number, demonstrating a principled pipeline from unsupervised program discovery to causal effect estimation. The mintMR framework (Xiang et al., American Journal of Human Genetics 2024) uses multi-view learning to model latent tissue-specific indicators of disease relevance, iterating between per-gene MR and joint tissue relevance learning. Genomic SEM (Grotzinger et al. 2019) pioneered incorporating latent genetic factors into MR-based causal networks. **No published work has yet performed MR specifically on NMF-derived protein modules from population proteomics** — a clear and high-value opportunity given the availability of pQTL data from the UK Biobank Pharma Proteomics Project.

**Transfer learning** for factorization has matured through tools like projectR (Sharma et al., Bioinformatics 2020), which projects learned NMF/PCA patterns from source datasets onto target datasets with statistical significance testing. ProjectR has been used to transfer CoGAPS NMF patterns across species, tissues, and data platforms — including from scRNA-seq to spatial transcriptomics. The single-cell Embedded Topic Model (scETM, Zhao et al., Nature Communications 2021) achieves zero-shot cross-tissue and cross-species transfer learning by combining a transferable neural encoder with an interpretable linear decoder, representing a major advance for applying topic models across cohorts.

**Cross-platform proteomics harmonization** remains a formidable challenge. Eldjarn et al. (Nature 2023) showed that Olink and SomaScan have only **modest cross-platform correlations** (bimodal distribution with peaks at r~0 and r~0.8). Olink demonstrated higher cis-pQTL support (72% vs. 43% of assays), suggesting better target specificity, while SomaScan showed lower measurement variability (median CV 9.9% vs. 16.5%). A comprehensive 7-platform comparison (Ahadi et al., bioRxiv 2025) found that only 7 proteins (including IGFBP2, IGFBP3) were consistently identified as age-associated across all platforms. The timsTOF HT mass spectrometry platform (Vitko et al., Journal of Proteome Research 2024) now achieves >7,000 protein groups in 5-minute gradients with >2-fold improvement in reproducible precursors over its predecessor, making population-scale DIA-MS proteomics feasible — but **no latent factor analysis has been published on DIA-MS population data**.

For multi-omics integration, the Cantini et al. benchmark (Nature Communications 2021) compared 9 joint dimensionality reduction methods across TCGA cancer types and found that **MCIA offered the most consistently strong performance**, while intNMF excelled at clustering and MOFA at clinical annotation. MEFISTO (Velten et al., Nature Methods 2022) extends MOFA with Gaussian process priors for temporal/spatial structure, enabling smooth factor trajectories — directly applicable to longitudinal proteomics. Tensor factorization methods (MONTI, tcam) naturally capture multi-way relationships (genes × samples × conditions × omics) that matrix methods collapse.

## Technical failure modes that practitioners must navigate

### Identifiability is the foundational challenge

NMF solutions are inherently non-unique: given any valid factorization WH, the product (WC)(C⁻¹H) for any non-negative invertible matrix C is equally valid. This goes far beyond trivial permutation/scaling ambiguity. The Donoho-Stodden separability condition (NIPS 2003) establishes identifiability when each factor has at least one "anchor" feature unique to it — equivalent to the anchor-word assumption in topic models (Arora et al. 2012). The more relaxed sufficiently scattered condition (SSC) of Fu et al. (IEEE Signal Processing 2019) requires factor weights to be well-spread in the non-negative orthant, enabling volume-minimization NMF to achieve identifiability under milder assumptions. However, checking SSC is NP-hard in general. **ICA achieves identifiability through a fundamentally different mechanism**: if source signals are statistically independent and at most one is Gaussian, the mixing matrix is identified up to permutation and sign (Comon 1994). This makes ICA particularly well-suited when biological processes are truly independent — but if log-transformation pushes data toward Gaussianity, ICA's identifiability guarantees weaken.

### Rank selection lacks a universal solution

Choosing the number of factors K remains more art than science. cNMF's approach — stability of NMF components across hundreds of replicates, measured by silhouette score — is currently the practical gold standard for scRNA-seq, though Kotliar et al. acknowledge "there is no universally definitive criteria for choosing K." The cophenetic correlation coefficient (Brunet et al., PNAS 2004) works for small datasets but **fails for noisy high-dimensional RNA-seq data** (Zeng et al., Bioinformatics 2017). MOFA's ARD prior offers the most principled approach: it automatically prunes factors by driving irrelevant weight precisions to infinity, effectively performing model selection within the inference procedure. For topic models, held-out perplexity and topic coherence scores are standard but often disagree with each other.

### Data type determines method suitability

The most critical technical challenge for applying transcriptomics-developed methods to proteomics is **the mismatch between data types**. scRNA-seq produces non-negative integer counts — natural for NMF and Poisson/multinomial topic models. Olink NPX values are log₂-scaled continuous measurements that can be negative (representing proteins below detection controls, typically ranging from ~−2 to ~15). Mass spectrometry intensities are continuous and can become negative after batch correction. Applying standard NMF to Olink data requires either exponentiating to linear scale (creating extreme dynamic range dominated by high-abundance proteins), shifting by a constant (distorting relative distances), or splitting into positive/negative parts (doubling dimensionality). **MOFA, semi-NMF, PCA-based methods, and factor analysis are more appropriate** for continuous proteomics data because they impose no non-negativity constraint.

Overfitting manifests distinctly in high-dimensional low-sample proteomics. With ~7,000 proteins measured on ~500 individuals, standard NMF with K=10 factors estimates 75,000 parameters. Kotliar et al. specifically noted that "NMF often yields low but non-zero usages for many GEPs even though we expect most cells to express a small number of GEPs," attributing this to overfitting. MOFA's two-level Bayesian regularization — ARD for factor-level and spike-and-slab for feature-level sparsity — provides built-in protection, automatically determining effective model complexity from the data.

Batch effects, if uncorrected, will dominate the first several factors. For proteomics, Olink NPX data requires plate-level and batch-level normalization (intensity normalization, bridging samples) before any factorization. MOFA's multi-group framework and LIGER's integrative NMF offer model-based alternatives to pre-hoc correction, explicitly modeling which variation is shared across batches versus batch-specific — a more principled approach than removing variation before analysis.

## Five gaps that define the frontier

**First, cNMF has never been applied to population-scale plasma proteomics.** Despite being the gold-standard method for gene program discovery in scRNA-seq — adopted by the pan-cancer meta-program analyses of Gavish et al. (Nature 2023) and dozens of tumor atlas studies — cNMF has not been used to discover latent protein programs from SomaScan, Olink, or mass spectrometry data. The PubMed search "consensus NMF proteomics plasma population decomposition" returns zero results. The UK Biobank VAE study (Zhuang et al.) and MOFA study represent initial forays, but a systematic application of cNMF (adapted for continuous data), LDA, and ICA to the same population proteomics dataset — benchmarked against each other and against the supervised organ-clock factors — has not been attempted.

**Second, no cross-platform program transfer has been validated.** Despite >1,850 proteins overlapping between Olink and SomaScan, and growing DIA-MS cohort data, no study has discovered latent programs on one platform and projected them onto another using projectR-like approaches. This is essential for establishing whether discovered programs reflect biology rather than platform artifacts. The seven-protein consistency finding from Ahadi et al.'s 7-platform comparison (only IGFBP2, IGFBP3, and five others were age-associated across all platforms) underscores the urgency: if individual protein associations are this platform-dependent, validating at the program level becomes critical.

**Third, the supervised-versus-unsupervised comparison has never been performed on the same data.** No paper has applied NMF/cNMF/MOFA to the same SomaScan or Olink aging datasets used by Oh et al. and directly compared the discovered programs with the GTEx-labeled organ models. Do unsupervised factors recapitulate organ-specific signals? Do they discover cross-organ programs (systemic inflammation, proteostasis decline, SASP) that the supervised approach fragments? These questions remain completely unanswered.

**Fourth, DIA-MS population proteomics is a blank canvas for latent factor analysis.** The timsTOF HT platform now achieves >7,000 proteins from plasma with high reproducibility, and Seer Proteograph enrichment pushes this further. Population-scale DIA-MS cohorts are emerging, but no NMF, MOFA, or topic model analysis has been published on this data type. DIA-MS is uniquely valuable because it avoids the epitope-binding specificity concerns that plague affinity-based platforms — programs discovered in DIA-MS data would have orthogonal validation potential.

**Fifth, the connection from unsupervised program discovery to causal inference is almost entirely unexplored in proteomics.** The UK Biobank Pharma Proteomics Project provides pQTL data for ~2,900 Olink proteins in >54,000 individuals (Sun et al., Nature 2023). If NMF or MOFA were applied to this same proteomic data to discover latent programs, those programs' genetic instruments (constructed from pQTLs of constituent proteins) could be used in Mendelian randomization to test causal effects on disease outcomes. The MVMR-BFA framework of Zhang et al. (2026) provides the statistical machinery. This pipeline — unsupervised program discovery → genetic instrumentation → causal inference — represents arguably the highest-impact application of matrix factorization to population proteomics, and it has not been attempted.

---

## Conclusion

The unsupervised decomposition of population-scale plasma proteomics represents a conspicuous methodological gap with transformative potential. The transcriptomics community has demonstrated that cNMF, topic models, and MOFA can discover biologically meaningful programs at scale — programs that predict treatment response, reveal cell-state transitions, and generate mechanistic hypotheses. Meanwhile, the plasma proteomics field has anchored itself to a supervised organ-labeling paradigm that, while clinically powerful, constrains discovery to predetermined anatomical categories. The methods, the data, and the analytical infrastructure (pQTLs, multi-platform cohorts, causal inference frameworks) all exist. What remains is the execution: applying adapted versions of cNMF, MOFA, and topic models to the >50,000-individual UK Biobank proteomics datasets, benchmarking discovered programs against organ clocks, validating across Olink/SomaScan/DIA-MS platforms, and connecting latent programs to causal disease mechanisms through Mendelian randomization. The field needs not just better methods but the willingness to let the data — rather than the GTEx atlas — define the vocabulary of circulating protein biology.
